# Religion of Vharos

## AT A GLANCE

Religion in Vharos is immediate, public, and dangerous. People pray for rain sealants, healthy beasts, safe tunnel shifts, and one more dawn after convoy duty. Temples are not distant philosophical halls; they are ration brokers, infirmaries, legal witnesses, and militia sponsors.

The rise of Ascendants has disrupted every established faith. Some churches treat awakened sparks as divine confirmation, others as blasphemous theft, and many as both depending on politics, region, and season. A priest in Vharos must be theologian, quartermaster, and intelligence officer in equal measure.

### Canon Constraints

- Religion must remain playable, factional, and tied to survival systems.
- Faith institutions can bless and burden PCs through obligations, not only boons.
- Ascendant theology remains contested; no single doctrine is universally accepted.
- Kharad-Vul's siege and Underdark pressure influence doctrine and church policy.

## THE DIVINE LANDSCAPE OF VHAROS

No single empire-faith rules Vharos. The divine field is organized into compact but influential traditions called mantles: practical clusters of worship centered on related needs. Most communities host shrines from multiple mantles, and most households keep mixed practice.

### Canon Facts

- The four dominant mantles are Forge and Stone, Rain and Root, Ember and Oath, and Veil and Bone.
- Civil governments commonly grant temple charters in exchange for measurable services: healing quotas, cistern upkeep, and convoy support.
- Public rites are often multi-faith by necessity, especially in mixed caravan towns.
- Ascendant manifestations are recorded in clerical ledgers and used as legal evidence in some tribunal systems.

### Uncertain Reports

- Some senior clergy claim the mantles are masks worn by a far smaller number of true powers.
- It is said a pre-collapse fifth mantle, tied to stars and memory, is re-emerging through dream cults in the interior.

### Adventure Hooks

- A charter dispute shuts down three temple infirmaries unless the party arbitrates a mantle-right boundary.
- A stolen clerical ledger appears to prove a famous Ascendant miracle was staged for political gain.

## MAJOR FAITHS AND ASCENDANT CULTS

The following are the most influential organized faith networks currently active in campaign-relevant regions.

### Temple of the Anvil Father (AHN-vil)

Stone, labor discipline, oath-forging, and civic endurance. Strong in Kharad-Vul and allied holdfasts.

- **Canon facts**
  - Oversees forge oaths, tunnel safety rites, and bell-watch consecrations.
  - Authorized to witness high-value contracts in dwarven jurisdictions.
  - Maintains militant deacon orders for breach-response in siege zones.
- **Uncertain reports**
  - Dissenters say senior prelates are selecting favored Ascendants as future rulers under guise of prophecy.
- **Adventure hooks**
  - A breach-response relic goes missing before a major tunnel evacuation.
  - Rival deacon houses demand the party validate competing miracle claims.

### Choir of the Verdant Mother (VAIR-dant)

Rain law, growth rites, medicinal botany, and negotiated coexistence with the jungle.

- **Canon facts**
  - Controls many sanctioned herb gardens and spore-safe treatment houses.
  - Trains route-blessers who accompany caravans into green-line corridors.
  - Enforces taboo against reckless canopy burning in settled basins.
- **Uncertain reports**
  - Hunters claim hidden choir circles bargain directly with apex predators during drought years.
- **Adventure hooks**
  - A blight cure requires access to forbidden marsh groves guarded by choir hardliners.
  - A respected route-blesser vanishes after denouncing a major merchant patron.

### Ember Throne Chaplaincies

State-adjacent priesthoods tied to civic oaths, military command legitimacy, and sanctioned punishment.

- **Canon facts**
  - Conduct investiture rites for marshals, magistrates, and convoy wardens.
  - Keep public oath archives used in debt and treason trials.
  - Bless war standards and maintain funeral rolls for siege dead.
- **Uncertain reports**
  - There are whispers that some chaplaincies sell absolution clauses to wealthy houses.
- **Adventure hooks**
  - A forged investiture seal appears on mobilization orders.
  - A funeral roll names someone still alive and now wanted by three jurisdictions.

### Keepers of the Veil Lantern

Guardians of funerary passage, ancestor witness, and anti-necromantic warding.

- **Canon facts**
  - Administer corpse law and burial transit between districts during crisis.
  - Operate lantern wards in plague, siege, and mass-casualty conditions.
  - Condemn spirit-binding commerce but permit ancestor consultation under strict rite.
- **Uncertain reports**
  - Some lantern houses are rumored to keep sanctioned ghost archives for intelligence work.
- **Adventure hooks**
  - A lantern ward fails and the dead begin repeating last-command phrases from an old war.
  - A noble family offers great payment to suppress an ancestor testimony.

### Ascendant Cult Constellations

Loose but fast-growing movements organized around living spark-bearers, divine techniques, and promised apotheosis.

- **Canon facts**
  - Cults form around visible miracles, battlefield survival, and redistribution promises.
  - Most cults fracture quickly into doctrine circles once their leader gains influence.
  - Established temples attempt either co-option or formal censure within two chains of a cult's rise.
- **Uncertain reports**
  - Rumor holds that one hidden network can transfer fragmentary spark-power among initiates.
- **Adventure hooks**
  - Two cult branches demand the party authenticate incompatible prophecies from the same Ascendant.
  - A cult-funded granary opens in famine season, forcing city authorities to choose tolerance or crackdown.

## RITUAL LIFE, TABOOS, AND SACRED CALENDAR

Ritual in Vharos is tied to environmental pressure and civic order. Holy days coincide with weather risks, convoy turnover, siege milestones, and remembrance cycles. Most rites require witnesses, making religion central to public trust.

### Canon Facts

- Ember Nights close the civil year with debt audits, absolution petitions, and oath renewal.
- Rainfast Rite marks storm onset and includes mandatory cistern blessings plus roof-ward inspections.
- Chainbreak Vigil honors siege escapes and names the dead before command transfer ceremonies.
- First Ash Supper each floodmark formally reconciles caravan disputes before new route contracts are signed.

### Uncertain Reports

- It is said that skipping Chainbreak names in a hold-city allows the unremembered dead to return as omen voices.
- Some caravan elders insist certain route blessings only work when led by mixed-faith crews.

### Adventure Hooks

- During Rainfast inspections, a shrine ledger reveals years of falsified ward certifications.
- A Chainbreak Vigil is targeted by assassins who need one witness-line erased from public memory.

## CHURCH POWER, LAW, AND CONFLICT

Temple authority in Vharos is transactional but formidable. Charter rights, healing access, burial control, and oath witnessing give churches practical leverage over markets and courts. Open war between major faiths is rare; proxy struggle through guilds, militias, and legal process is constant.

### Canon Facts

- Temple-chartered healhouses can impose service priorities during declared emergency.
- Clerical witness marks are admissible in most civil and military tribunals.
- Church militias are legal in frontier zones if registered and bell-accountable.
- Councils frequently balance competing faith blocs by rotating festival authority and ration oversight.

### Uncertain Reports

- Several magistrates suspect an interfaith black ledger tracks bribery, coercion, and hidden pacts among top prelates.
- There are stories of a sealed concord that forbids temples from publicly declaring any Ascendant a true god.

### Adventure Hooks

- A council election turns on which temple receives cistern authority for the dry chains.
- The party is hired to escort neutral auditors through districts where both church militias claim lawful command.

## HERESIES, SCHISMS, AND FORBIDDEN DEVOTIONS

Heresy in Vharos is often less about doctrine than risk profile. Practices are condemned when they threaten public order, corpse law, food supply, or oath credibility. Still, desperation births new sects each season, and some become tomorrow's orthodoxy.

### Canon Facts

- Bone-market rites that trade memory or blood for necromantic service are banned in most chartered cities.
- Unsanctioned spark-harvesting, including post-mortem extraction attempts, is universally illegal where enforceable.
- Several lawful churches tolerate controlled ancestor invocation but forbid bodily reanimation.
- Schisms most often occur over Ascendant legitimacy and redistribution doctrine.

### Uncertain Reports

- Fringe pamphlets claim an ancient rite can sever a divine spark without killing the host.
- A persistent rumor names a hidden convent beneath Kharad-Vul that shelters excommunicated miracle workers.

### Adventure Hooks

- A forbidden devotional text surfaces in a market district and both censors and cultists race to seize it first.
- A respected priest is accused of spark-harvesting, but evidence suggests political framing.

## GAME INFORMATION

Use this section to apply religion mechanically at the table without reducing faith to simple buffs.

- **Temple standing:** Track party standing separately with major faith blocs (favored, tolerated, censured, anathematized).
- **Ritual obligations:** Powerful blessings should carry explicit duties, deadlines, or public witness requirements.
- **Holy day pressure:** Run festival sessions as high-density social and faction scenes with legal and theological stakes.
- **Ascendant doctrine conflict:** When characters display divine power, trigger at least one institutional reaction (recruitment, censure, patronage, or legal challenge).
- **Corpse law and burial control:** In siege or plague arcs, funerary authority should materially affect morale, rumor flow, and undead risk.
- **Heresy as plot engine:** Use schisms to generate investigations, trials, defections, and covert alliances rather than only combat encounters.
